Coffee Weather Analysis

This project explores whether weather and seasonality influence coffee buying behavior across three NYC neighborhoods: Lower Manhattan, Hell’s Kitchen, and Astoria.

By combining transaction-level coffee sales data with daily weather metrics, I examined how temperature and precipitation related to product demand, store-level sales, and neighborhood purchasing patterns.

Project Snapshot

  • Business Question

    Do weather and seasonal conditions change coffee buying behavior, and do those effects vary by neighborhood?

  • Timeframe

    January 2023 – June 2023

  • Tools

    BigQuery, SQL, Excel, data cleaning, aggregation, and dashboard design

  • Locations

    Lower Manhattan, Hell’s Kitchen, Astoria

Overview

  1. Objective

    I wanted to understand whether daily temperature and rainfall were associated with changes in coffee shop sales, and whether those relationships differed across neighborhoods and product categories.

  2. Data

    The project combined coffee transaction data, including date, quantity, pricing, store location, product category, product type, and product detail, with daily weather data including temperature, rain, snowfall, and snow depth.

  3. Approach

    I cleaned the raw sales and weather tables, converted hourly weather records into daily summaries, joined the datasets on transaction date, and created SQL aggregations to analyze store-level and category-level patterns.

Analysis Highlights

  1. 1. Sales Trends by Store

    I compared daily sales across the three neighborhoods to identify differences in demand patterns over time.

  2. 2. Temperature vs. Sales

    I analyzed whether warmer or colder days were associated with shifts in daily sales and purchase quantity.

  3. 3. Rainy vs. Non-Rainy Days

    I grouped days by rainfall presence to compare store performance under different weather conditions.

  4. 4. Product Mix by Weather

    I explored how product categories and types behaved across different weather scenarios to identify weather-sensitive demand.

Key Findings

  • Temperature mattered more than snow

    Temperature showed a clearer relationship with sales behavior than snow-related metrics in the January–June period.

  • Rain effects varied by location

    Rainy days were associated with different sales patterns across neighborhoods, suggesting local demand sensitivity.

  • Product demand was not uniform

    Some product categories appeared more weather-sensitive than others, indicating an opportunity for targeted promotions.

  • Neighborhood context matters

    The three store locations showed different purchasing patterns, suggesting neighborhood-level strategy could improve performance.

Business Recommendations

  1. Weather-triggered promotions

    Use forecast-based campaigns to promote relevant items depending on rainy, cold, or warm conditions.

  2. Location-specific planning

    Adjust promotions, staffing, and merchandising strategies based on neighborhood-level demand behavior.

  3. Inventory optimization

    Align product emphasis and stock planning with seasonal and weather-sensitive category trends.

Limitations

  1. This analysis covered only January through June 2023 and did not account for holidays, promotions, or weekday/weekend effects.

  2. Weather data was aggregated at the city level rather than perfectly store-specific.
